You see every vendor bill that crosses your desk. What you don't see is the cost of not paying it.

## Spending Millions to Save Thousands

There's a quiet tax on innovation in most organizations. It is not in your cloud bill, your vendor contracts, or your quarterly forecasts. It lives in the hundreds of internal tools, frameworks, and “quick custom solutions” your teams build for problems that were already solved somewhere else. Each one feels small. Together, they become a drag on execution, hiring, risk, and, ultimately, growth.

When a team avoids a $30,000 software license by building their own version, it feels like a saving. But that "saving" typically costs multiple developer-years to build, maintain, and support. At fully loaded salaries, you are spending six or seven figures to avoid a five-figure bill. This is organizational inefficiency hiding under the surface.

## The Costs You Cannot See

Vendor invoices are visible. Developer time spent rebuilding solved problems is not. It shows up as slower delivery, missed deadlines, and teams that seem busy but are not moving the product forward.

Ask how much of your engineering budget goes toward solving problems someone else has already solved. The answer may surprise you.

## Focus Is Your Most Valuable Asset

Every developer rebuilding infrastructure is a developer not building the features that differentiate your business. The true cost of "building it ourselves" is not just what you spend; it is what you do not ship.

This is not about outsourcing everything. It is about **allocating scarce engineering capacity to where it yields strategic return**, and reusing what the rest of the world has already battle-tested.

**Guard your focus.** Ask your teams, in every proposal:

* Which existing products, platforms, or open-source tools were considered?
* Why were they rejected?
* What is the long-term cost of owning this ourselves?

You are not micromanaging. You are **guarding the organization’s focus and balance sheet**.

## Your Dependencies Should Be Healthy

Every custom system is a promise: someone will understand it, maintain it, and extend it in five years. Turnover breaks that promise.

Established technologies come with:

* A hiring pool of people who already know them.
* Documentation, books, courses, and community expertise.
* Consultants and vendors who can step in when needed.

When your business depends on a piece of software, you want it maintained by a funded team, not a single individual. A well-funded dependency with a growing community and predictable revenue is a safer bet than a free tool that could be abandoned tomorrow.

Paying for critical dependencies is not an expense. It is risk management.

## Your Risks Should Be Predictable

When something critical fails at 2 AM, you want:

* A known product with support options
* Searchable incident reports from other companies
* A clear upgrade and patch path

You do not want an on-call engineer staring at a custom internal system built by someone who left the company five years ago.

## AI Works Better With Standard Systems

AI assistants and copilots work best with widely used technologies. Models have been trained on **thousands of examples** of standard tools and frameworks. Using those models to work with **homegrown, undocumented frameworks** wastes tokens and increases risks. By using established technologies, you increase **developer leverage**; AI tooling can write, refactor, and troubleshoot more of your code base. That compounds productivity gains that your competitors may already be enjoying.

## Where Custom Still Matters

“Use What Works” is not a commandment to never build. Custom solutions make sense when:

* The problem is **core to your strategy** (pricing engine, risk models, domain-specific workflows).
* Existing solutions **clearly cannot meet your requirements**.
* Owning the implementation gives you **real strategic or regulatory advantage**.

The key difference: Custom work is **the exception, not the default**, and is **explicitly justified in business terms**.

Ask yourself  "what is the strategic sentence I can say to the board that justifies us building this instead of using what already exists?” If you don't have a good answer, you should not be building it.

## Make It Part of the Process

For major initiatives, add a **Use What Works check** to your approval process:

- Does this initiative rely on **proven products and platforms** where possible?
- How much engineering capacity is reserved for **differentiating work** vs **infrastructure reinvention**?
- If we are building custom, what is the **multi-year ownership cost** and who owns it?

Tie this to your existing governance mechanisms:

- Architecture review boards
- Portfolio councils
- Capital allocation committees

The point is not more bureaucracy. The point is **making invisible technical risk visible in business conversations**.

## Reward Reuse, Not Reinvention

Leaders get what they measure and reward. It's far too common to see teams rewarded for launching new internal platforms, and penalised for choosing boring, proven ones.

Flip that. Celebrate teams that **ship faster by reusing proven tools**. Reward leaders who **retire** custom systems in favor of simpler, more sustainable ones. Make “leveraging existing capabilities” part of performance criteria for technical leadership roles.

When career incentives line up with “Use What Works,” your culture starts enforcing the vision for you.

## Capture Data and Measure Progress

You do not need a huge dashboard. A few simple metrics tell you if you are moving in the right direction:

- **Percentage of systems using standard platforms** for auth, logging, deployment, monitoring
- **Number of internally maintained frameworks or platforms** (aim to reduce over time)
- **Time to onboard a new engineer** into a typical product team
- **Incidents tied to custom internal infrastructure**

If these indicators improve, your **Use What Works** vision is starting to have real operational impact.

## Signs You're Not Using What Works

You may recognize some of these symptoms:

- Every product team has **its own way** to deploy, log, or monitor.
- New hires spend months learning **internal frameworks** before they can ship value.
- Incidents routinely trace back to **custom infrastructure** rather than standard, vendor-supported tools.
- Architects are overwhelmed reviewing **one-off bespoke designs** instead of patterns.
- Engineers are more excited about **building platforms** than solving customer problems.

These are not just technical smells. They are **business risk signals**.

## "Use What Works" Is a Leadership Choice

“Use What Works” is not a slogan for engineers. It is a **strategic position**:

- You choose to invest deeply where you win.
- You choose to stand on the shoulders of proven solutions everywhere else.
- You choose predictability over vanity, leverage over reinvention, and sustainability over brittle cleverness.

Your teams will continue to propose custom solutions. That is their nature and, in the right places, their strength. Your role is to **set the boundary**:

*“We build where it differentiates. We use what works everywhere else.”*

Get that right, and technology becomes what you always wanted it to be: a force multiplier for your strategy, not a hidden tax on it.
